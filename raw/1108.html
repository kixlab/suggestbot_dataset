How much money would recreational marijuana really generate in Connecticut?

By Daniela Altimari
| Hartford Courant |
Jan 17, 2019 | 12:26 PM

How much tax revenue will legalizing marijuana generate for cash-starved Connecticut?

That depends on who you ask.

Advertisement

Kebra Smith-Bolden, a registered nurse from New Haven who owns a cannabis business, sees a potential boon to fund schools and programs for seniors and help rebuild urban communities that have been hurt by the historic inequities of the war on drugs.

"The revenue that cannabis legalization can bring in is substantial and there’s enough to go around,'' Smith-Bolden said. “We can do a lot of things with that revenue.”

Senate President Pro Tem Martin Looney, a New Haven Democrat and a strong supporter of taxing and regulating pot, agrees: “It would be a significant new revenue source for the state of Connecticut at a time when there is general reluctance to raise taxes.”

Smith-Bolden and other legalization advocates say the retail sale of marijuana could generate as much as $180 million annually in new tax dollars, though Looney said “The exact amount we’ll take in is going to be determined by the tax structure that we chose.”

Paul Kirchberg, executive director of Connecticut NORML, which supports ending prohibition on marijuana, said policy makers tend to underestimate the tax benefits of legalization.

"If all the people in favor of legalization went out and bought $1,000 in cannabis products a year, we would reach that $180 million number fairly quickly,'' he said. “It’s very easy to spend $1,000 a year--a single joint offering can cost $30.”

While $180 million sounds like a big number, it’s far less than the sum generated by the cigarette tax, which raised $32,653,033 in the month of December alone, according to the Department of Revenue Services.

And the marijuana tax receipts would hardly dent the state’s $1.7 billion deficit in the 2019-20 fiscal year, a gap that’s projected to grow to $2.3 billion in 2020-21.

Critics of legalization say the sunny tax projections put forth by cannabis supporters don’t add up. "This is not a money-maker by any means,'' said Kevin A. Sabet, president of Smart Approaches to Marijuana, a national group opposed to legalizing marijuana for recreational use. “Anyone looking for a big windfall is going to be very disappointed.”

Just look at other states that have already legalized marijuana, Sabet said. "In state after state, marijuana legalization is not worth the cost,'' he said. "Revenues account less than 1 percent of any state budget.''

Advertisement

While the federal ban on marijuana remains in effect, a patchwork of policies have sprung up in states. The District of Columbia and 10 states – Alaska, California, Colorado, Maine, Massachusetts, Michigan, Nevada, Oregon, Vermont and Washington – have legalized cannabis for recreational use by adults.

Those states have established various tax structures and one of them, Vermont, doesn’t permit the commercial sale of cannabis products. (Vermont allows those over 21 to possess up to 1 ounce of marijuana, as well as two mature marijuana plants and four immature plants, but it has no mechanism for the retail sale of marijuana. The state is currently reviewing a regulatory structure for commercial sales and is considering a 26 percent tax rate.)

[Politics] Sen. Fasano to Gov. Lamont: Show us the savings »

In Massachusetts, where voters approved the sale of recreational marijuana in a 2016 referendum, $2.2 million worth of gummies, vape cartridges and other cannabis product were sold in the first five days of legalization in November, generating $376,995 in state sales taxes, according to the Cannabis Control Commission. The towns of Leicester and Northampton, home to the state’s first two dispensaries, earned another $66,528 in local taxes.

Colorado, the first state to legalize marijuana for no-questions asked recreational use, is often held up as a case study on the enormous revenue potential of pot. In 2014, the state netted $67,594,323 in marijuana-related taxes, licenses and fees, according to the Colorado Department of Revenue.

By 2018, the number had grown to $266,529,637. Over the five years that taxes have been collected, Colorado has raised more than $900 million. The state is home to about 5.6 million people, about 2 million more than Connecticut.

In Washington state, which followed Colorado in allowing adult recreational use, consumers are assessed a 37 percent tax on retail marijuana sales. The state netted $315 million in marijuana tax revenue in the fiscal year ended on June 30, 2017.

It would be a significant new revenue source for the state of Connecticut at a time when there is general reluctance to raise taxes.

Looney said he would like to see Connecticut follow the Colorado model, which assess a 15 percent excise tax on the average market rate of retail marijuana. "Colorado has the most aggressive tax structure and that’s the one I’d like to see us follow,'' he said. “New York will be in the market very soon so we have to look at what level of taxation there is on our borders.”

Kirchberg of NORML said taxing marijuana at 6.35 percent--the same rate as other retail products--would generate $100 million annually for the state.

The legislature’s non-partisan fiscal office in 2017 offered a more sober assessment, projecting that legalizing and taxing marijuana would bring in more than $30 million in tax dollars in the first full year.

Critics such as Saban say any tax boon would be offset by the cost of establishing a regulatory system to oversee the marijuana industry.

He also predicted home growers and the black market of illegal sales will continue to thrive, as consumers seek a tax-free alternative to retail shops.

"In legal states like California, the market is bigger than ever,'' Saban said. “Anyone who thinks all those drug dealers are going to become dentists will be sorely disappointed.”

[Politics] Connecticut, New York in court battle over dumping in Long Island Sound »

And, critics say, the revenue-boosting opportunities of legalizing marijuana will be diluted as more states jump into the market. One state on Connecticut’s border--Massachusetts--has already jumped into the market and two others--New York and Rhode Island--are widely expected to do so soon. (A study by the Tax Foundation estimated that tax collections from marijuana could generate $5.3 billion annually if all states taxed the drug at a rate of 15 percent.)

Rhode Island Gov. Gina Raimondo included a proposal to legalize recreational marijuana in her budget. The Democrat estimates the state could net $6.5 million in revenue in the 2019-20 fiscal year, with the first shops opening as soon as next January. Citing health and safety concerns, Raimondo’s plan would ban home-growing, closing off a lower-cost and tax-free way to access the drug.

Despite the appeal of marijuana as a source of revenue, some policy analysts advise caution. A study released in July by The Pew Charitable Trusts found various factors, from the difficulty of setting a tax rate to the instability of the market, contribute to that volatility.

"All of these variables make the long-term outlook for marijuana tax revenue hazy at best,'' the study concluded. “The growth of the industry and what that path might look like are challenging to predict.”

But supporters of legalization say the tax benefit is not only one reason to support the retail sale of marijuana.

"The ban has proved to be futile,'' Looney said. “People are able to get marijuana if they want it. Prohibition is not sustainable.”
